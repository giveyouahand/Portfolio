Context
Specialty drugs represent a small share of total pharmacy utilization but carry significantly higher costs per claim. The organization identified an opportunity to commercialize a specialty pharmacy competitive bidding capability, owned and operated by our PBM partner, as a standalone product offering. By packaging this capability with the right enrollment, implementation, and operational infrastructure, the health plan could bring a differentiated product to market that helped members access the lowest available specialty drug price while generating a new fee-based revenue stream.
Problem
While the PBM owned the bidding mechanism itself, the health plan had no internal infrastructure to sell, enroll, or operationalize the product. There was no defined process for sales teams to implement the benefit, no audit capability to verify enrollment accuracy, and no established workflow to ensure fees were correctly routed to the pharmacy book of business. Without these, the PBM capability could not be brought to market.
My role
I served as the senior program manager responsible for defining the product, building the internal infrastructure to support it, and ensuring ongoing operational integrity. I also mentored the PM on this product through their first solo ownership experience, providing guidance on stakeholder management, prioritization, and delivery throughout the program.
Approach
I led product definition by translating the PBM's bidding capability into a clearly scoped benefit that sales teams could confidently position and implement. I designed enrollment and implementation workflows that enabled consistent, accurate setup across commercial accounts. Working with legal, compliance, clinical, and technology stakeholders, I ensured the product met regulatory requirements and integrated cleanly with existing systems. I also established audit processes to verify enrollment accuracy and confirm that fees were properly flowing to the pharmacy book of business, a critical control given the fee-based revenue model.
Obstacles
The program required managing complexity across all major dimensions simultaneously, including regulatory and compliance requirements, competing priorities across commercial and clinical stakeholders, technical integration challenges, and ongoing timeline pressure. A key challenge was maintaining clarity about the boundary between what the PBM owned and what the health plan needed to build, ensuring the right work happened on the right side without duplicating effort or creating gaps.
Outcome

The product is on track to exceed $500K in revenue by end of year 1, with new groups continuing to enroll throughout the year. Members are benefiting from access to competitive specialty drug pricing through the PBM bidding process. The program is actively expanding to additional lines of business, further extending the revenue opportunity beyond the initial launch market.

Reflection
I would establish a more explicit operating model with the PBM earlier in the program, defining ownership boundaries, escalation paths, and data-sharing expectations before enrollment began. This would have reduced ambiguity during operational standup and made the audit process more efficient from the start.

Context
As program manager for a portfolio of medical products, I own the SOPs and implementation toolkits that guide medical coders and the SAM team through every stage of the product lifecycle, including account setup, billing, coding, renewal, and termination. These documents serve as the operational source of truth for the teams executing day to day, and I continuously improve them as gaps surface.
Problem
During a structured audit of Medication Finder, I identified a recurring pattern of fee allocation errors at account setup. PEPM fees were not being correctly split between the medical and pharmacy books of business, causing sustained misallocation to the wrong book of business across the portfolio. The errors were consistent enough to indicate a systemic root cause rather than isolated mistakes, but the source was not immediately obvious.
My role
I identified the gap through audit, traced the root cause across systems, drove a permanent fix with the underwriting team, led retroactive fee recovery for the affected book of business, and updated the SOP and training to lock in the corrected process. I also established an ongoing audit cadence as a sustained control layer going forward.
Approach
Tracing the error required investigating across billing, coding, and account management workflows until I identified the true source: a separate underwriting system that allowed fees to be embedded in a way that prevented proper allocation to individual books of business. Rather than working around the system, I partnered with the underwriting team to make a permanent configuration change, removing the option to embed fees in a way that obscured the split and ensuring products requiring separate line items would be set up correctly going forward. I then updated the SOP to reflect the corrected process, trained the SAM team directly, initiated retroactive recovery billing for the affected book of business, and implemented a structured audit cadence as an ongoing control.
Obstacles
The root cause was not visible at the surface level and required tracing the error across multiple systems before landing on the underwriting configuration as the source. Driving a permanent system change with a separate team required building alignment around a fix that affected their workflows. Recovering fees retroactively required careful coordination to apply corrections accurately without disrupting the book of business.
Outcome

Medication Finder saw a 33% increase in revenue correctly allocated to the appropriate book of business. Approximately $100K in misallocated fees were recovered. The permanent underwriting system change eliminated the source of the error entirely, significantly improving revenue tracking clarity and reducing setup errors for all products requiring separate fee line items going forward.

Reflection
I would engage the underwriting team earlier in the product launch process to review system configuration requirements before the first accounts go live. The root cause was upstream, and closing that gap at launch rather than post-audit would have prevented the misallocation entirely.

Context
As strategic product manager for a white-labeled medical savings product, I owned the UI/UX of the member-facing experience. The product helped members find savings on medical services and medications by connecting them to a vendor platform presented seamlessly as part of the health plan's own digital experience. Driving member registration and activation on this platform was essential to delivering on the product's value proposition.
Problem
Members accessing the product for the first time were ported out to the vendor site and immediately prompted to create a new account, despite already being signed into the health plan's main site. The experience felt inconsistent and confusing: members who believed they were navigating within a single platform were suddenly asked to register again. This friction caused significant drop-off at the registration step, with only 2% of members completing registration. The vendor's architecture at the time supported only one access pathway, leaving no immediate technical workaround.
My role
I led the end-to-end strategic pivot, from identifying the member experience breakdown to designing the multi-door access model to coordinating across legal, data, technology, and vendor teams to bring the solution to life. This was not a minor UX adjustment. It required rethinking how members could reach and authenticate into the vendor platform entirely.
Approach
Rather than accepting the single-door constraint as fixed, I reframed the problem: if members could not be onboarded smoothly at the moment of first access, we needed to create earlier and alternative pathways to get them registered before friction became a barrier. Working through legal and data teams, I designed a proactive email enrollment flow that allowed members to register ahead of their first use. I also worked with the vendor and internal technology teams to implement SSO, eliminating re-authentication from any entry point. In parallel, I expanded member access from a single link to multiple touchpoints including banners, additional links, and the email pathway, creating a multi-door model that met members where they were.
Obstacles
The vendor's original architecture supported only one access pathway, so expanding to a multi-door model required negotiating and coordinating changes on the vendor side. The proactive email enrollment flow required navigating data sharing and legal review to ensure member data was handled appropriately across the health plan and vendor boundary. Aligning all of these workstreams simultaneously while maintaining a coherent member experience required sustained cross-functional coordination over the full delivery cycle.
Outcome

Registration rate increased from 2% to 3.5%, a 75% relative improvement, following implementation. Members previously dropping off at the single-entry registration prompt now had multiple pathways to activate, and SSO eliminated the re-authentication barrier entirely. The registration increase directly unlocked benefit utilization, as activated members could now access the savings tools the product was designed to deliver.

A note on the numbers: registration rates for white-labeled vendor products in this category are characteristically low in absolute terms, as they represent a subset of eligible members who actively engage with a supplemental benefit. The meaningful signal here is the 75% relative lift, demonstrating that the friction, not the product, was suppressing activation.

Reflection
I would push for SSO and proactive enrollment pathways to be requirements at vendor contract or product launch rather than improvements made post-launch. The registration barrier was a predictable consequence of the single-door, re-authentication model. Establishing these standards upfront would have avoided the activation gap entirely and accelerated time to member value.

Context
The provider finder website had no scheduling capability despite it being a long-standing corporate strategy roadmap item. When leadership prioritized delivery, the program came with a hard constraint: funding would run out within a defined window, making on-time delivery non-negotiable. I led the program with a team of BAs, owning design, delivery, and implementation end to end.
Problem
Delivering a fully integrated scheduling feature within the funding window was not feasible. The website was powered by an external vendor, scheduling required real-time provider availability data from a separate clinical partner, and the solution needed to clear legal, data governance, behavioral health vendor, and engineering workstreams simultaneously. Attempting a full build in one pass would risk missing the deadline and losing the program entirely.
My role
I led the full program, scoping the MVP, managing delivery across all workstreams, and leading the BAs supporting execution. I made the strategic call to phase delivery and defined what belonged in MVP versus fast follow, ensuring phase 1 would meet the funding deadline while laying the architectural foundation for the full build.
Approach
I scoped the MVP around what was achievable within the constraint: partnering with a behavioral health vendor to supply clinician availability data and building a lightweight scheduling entry point that surfaced a "schedule now" button for providers with availability in the next two weeks. Members were ported to the vendor site through a streamlined registration flow designed specifically for appointment completion. I designed the MVP framework to be vendor-agnostic from the start, so any future scheduling partner providing the right data could be onboarded without rebuilding the integration layer. That decision directly enabled phase 2.
Phases
Phase 1 — MVP

"Schedule now" button surfaced for behavioral health providers with availability within 2 weeks. Members ported to vendor site via a dedicated registration flow. Delivered within funding window.
Phase 2 — Fast follow

Obstacles
Legal review became the critical path bottleneck. Establishing a net-new data sharing relationship with the behavioral health vendor required legal sign-off that threatened to compress the remaining delivery window to the point of jeopardy. I worked closely with legal to prioritize the review, framed the funding deadline as an organizational risk, and sequenced other workstreams to absorb the delay without losing ground. Keeping engineering and vendor coordination moving in parallel during the legal hold was the defining challenge of the program.
Outcome

The MVP was delivered within the funding window, realizing a corporate strategy roadmap item that had been deferred for years. Member scheduling activity increased following launch. The vendor-agnostic framework built in phase 1 directly enabled the website vendor to deliver fully integrated native scheduling in phase 2, turning a constrained MVP into a scalable platform capability.

Reflection
I would initiate legal review at program kickoff rather than after vendor selection. The data sharing agreement was always going to be required and the timeline was always going to be tight. Starting that workstream earlier would have removed the biggest single risk to on-time delivery before it became a crisis.
